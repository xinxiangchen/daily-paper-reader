Abstract: Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have become a promising paradigm for generalist robot manipulation, where visual-language representa-tions are used to condition continuous action generation. However, these repre-sentations are not explicitly optimized for action conditioning, leaving the action expert to bridge the gap between multimodal understanding and precise motor control. Recent action-reasoning methods introduce additional modules to gener-ate explicit action plans or action-space reasoning signals, demonstrating the ben-efit of action-level guidance but often requiring separate action-generation frame-works. We propose CAC-VLA , a Context-Gated Action Conditioning framework that learns a lightweight latent-action interface directly within the VLM. Instead of generating executable trajectories, CAC-VLA trains the VLM to predict coarse-to-fine latent actions, which are structured representations encoded from future action segments, and adaptively leverages them to condition the action expert via a context gate. This enables VLM-native action conditioning while calibrating the influence of latent-action guidance on expert action generation. Experiments on LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus demonstrate the effectiveness of CAC-VLA, achiev-ing 98.3% average success rate on LIBERO and 89.5% on LIBERO-Plus, sug-gesting that context-gated latent-action conditioning is an effective interface for continuous expert control. 

Keywords: VLA, Action Conditioning, Robotic Manipulation 

## 1 Introduction 

A robot that follows language instructions must not only understand what the task means, but also determine what physical actions should be executed. Recent Vision-Language-Action (VLA) mod-els [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] have made significant progress toward generalist robot manipulation by coupling pretrained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) [6, 7] with action experts that generate continuous robot commands. These models benefit from the semantic knowledge and multimodal reasoning capabilities of large-scale VLMs, enabling promising generalization across diverse objects, scenes, and instructions. However, most VLA architectures still use VLM representations as the primary interface between multimodal understanding and action generation. This leaves the transforma-tion from perception and language to control largely implicit: the action expert must infer both the task-level action structure and the fine-grained motor commands from representations that are not explicitly organized around actions. A growing line of work introduces intermediate reasoning signals to make this transformation more structured. Language-based approaches decompose tasks into subgoals or textual plans [4, 8], while vision-based approaches synthesize goal images or future observations [9, 10, 3]. More recent action-reasoning methods further move the intermediate representation closer to the control space

by generating action plans, reference trajectories, or action-space priors to guide downstream action prediction [11]. These works suggest that action generation can benefit from intermediate guidance that is more directly tied to the action space than generic visual-language representations. Never-theless, many existing action-reasoning methods obtain such guidance through additional action-generation or action-reasoning modules. Moreover, the resulting action guidance is often treated as a fixed conditioning signal, although its reliability and usefulness may vary with the current scene, task phase, and action-generation state. This motivates the technical question studied in this paper: beyond relying on implicit visual-language representations for action prediction, can we equip the VLM with an explicit latent-action interface, and can the action expert adaptively calibrate the influence of this action-structured guid-ance during continuous control? We address this question with CAC-VLA , a Context-Gated Action Conditioning framework. The core idea is to train the VLM to predict latent actions : encoded rep-resentations of future action segments that are not directly executed by the robot, but instead serve as intermediate action-structured guidance for expert action generation. In our implementation, we instantiate latent actions with raw latents from an ordered action tokenizer [12], which provides compact action representations with a coarse-to-fine structure. 

CAC-VLA constructs a lightweight latent-action interface within the VLA policy. Given visual observations and language instructions, dedicated VLM query tokens are trained to predict latent actions supervised by encoded future-action latents. These predictions serve as action-structured conditioning for the action expert, which remains responsible for generating the final continuous robot actions. Unlike methods that rely on standalone action reasoners or generators to synthesize explicit reference trajectories, CAC-VLA does not require the VLM or an auxiliary module to pro-duce executable action sequences as intermediate guidance. Instead, it learns compact latent-action representations that guide the expert during continuous action generation. A key challenge is that latent actions should not be treated as fixed commands. Since they are pre-dicted from multimodal context, they may be incomplete, noisy, or misaligned with the immediate control requirement. Moreover, the usefulness of coarse-to-fine latent actions can vary across task phases and action-generation states. To address this, CAC-VLA introduces a context gate that adap-tively calibrates the residual contribution of latent-action conditioning, rather than injecting it into the action expert with a fixed strength. We evaluate CAC-VLA on the LIBERO [13] and LIBERO-Plus [14] manipulation benchmarks. 

CAC-VLA achieves a 98.3% average success rate on LIBERO and 89.5% on LIBERO-Plus under supervised fine-tuning, showing that VLM-native latent-action prediction and context-gated expert conditioning provide effective action-structured guidance for continuous control. Ablation studies further demonstrate that both the latent-action horizon and the context gate are important for effec-tive expert conditioning. We also conduct an initial real-world tabletop pick-and-place evaluation to examine the real-world applicability of CAC-VLA beyond simulation. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We formulate latent-action conditioning as an intermediate interface for connecting VLM representations with continuous robot control. • We propose CAC-VLA , a context-gated action conditioning framework that uses VLM-predicted latent actions to adaptively guide the action expert. • We validate CAC-VLA on LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus, with ablations demonstrating the importance of latent-action horizon and context-gated conditioning. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Vision-Language-Action Models 

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models aim to build generalist robot policies that map visual ob-servations and language instructions to executable robot actions. Recent systems adapt pretrained 2vision-language backbones for robot control and pair them with action decoders, action heads, or action experts to produce executable actions [15, 16, 17, 18, 5, 4, 19, 20, 21]. These models demon-strate the effectiveness of combining large-scale visual-language representations with robot action generation. However, in many VLA architectures, visual-language representations remain the pri-mary interface between multimodal understanding and low-level control, leaving the action decoder or expert to infer action-relevant structure from features that are not explicitly organized for action conditioning. CAC-VLA is complementary to this line of work: it preserves the VLA paradigm while equipping the VLM with an explicit latent-action interface for action-structured expert conditioning. 

2.2 Action-Level Guidance 

Recent methods explore action-level guidance to provide intermediate signals closer to robot con-trol than language or visual reasoning. ACoT-VLA [11] formulates action-level reasoning as coarse action intents and introduces explicit and implicit action reasoners to provide reference trajectories and latent action priors for downstream action prediction. This suggests that action-structured guid-ance can help bridge the semantic-to-control gap in VLA policies. CAC-VLA shares this motivation but differs in how action guidance is obtained and used. Instead of introducing a standalone action generator or explicit reference-trajectory reasoner, CAC-VLA trains the VLM itself to predict latent actions. Moreover, rather than using action guidance as a fixed condition, CAC-VLA introduces a context gate to adaptively calibrate how strongly latent actions influence the action expert. 

2.3 Latent Actions 

Latent actions have recently been explored as compact intermediate abstractions for connecting visual-language understanding with robot control. For example, LAPA [22] learns discrete la-tent actions from unlabeled videos and pretrains VLA models to predict them before mapping the predictions to robot actions. UniVLA [23] derives task-centric latent actions from heterogeneous videos to support cross-embodiment policy learning with reduced reliance on action-labeled robot data. These works suggest that latent actions can provide action-level abstractions beyond standard visual-language representations. 

CAC-VLA also uses latent actions to improve VLA policy learning, but differs in how they are obtained and used. Rather than learning latent actions from unlabeled or heterogeneous videos for pretraining and transfer, CAC-VLA uses ordered action latents encoded from future robot action segments as supervision for VLM-native action conditioning. The predicted latent actions are not treated as an intermediate action space to be directly decoded; instead, they serve as action-structured conditioning for a continuous action expert, with context gating adaptively controlling their residual contribution during action generation.

3.3 Context-Gated Action Conditioning 

Although latent actions encode structured future-action information, they should be used as guid-ance rather than fixed commands. A simple strategy is to add or concatenate latent-action features with action hidden states, but such fixed fusion applies the same conditioning strength regardless of the expert layer or the current action-generation state. Since the action expert progressively re-fines continuous action representations, the usefulness of latent-action information may vary during generation. We therefore introduce Context-Gated Action Conditioning . The module first projects latent actions into expert-compatible conditioning tokens and uses cross-attention to retrieve latent-action infor-mation conditioned on the current action hidden states. It then applies a context gate to control how much of the retrieved update is injected into the expert through a residual connection. The conditioning source differs between training and inference. 3.4 Training and Inference 

The training objective combines the original action expert loss with the latent alignment loss: 

L = Lact + λalign Lalign (13) where Lact denotes the original flow-matching action expert loss and λalign balances latent-action supervision. During training, the frozen OAT tokenizer encodes future action segments as both alignment targets and expert conditioning sources. The VLM query tokens are trained to predict the same latent representation through Lalign . During inference, the OAT tokenizer is removed, and the expert is conditioned only on VLM-predicted latent actions. Thus, CAC-VLA uses future-action structure as training-time supervision while remaining VLM-native at deployment. 

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate CAC-VLA on standard simulation benchmarks to validate the effectiveness of VLM-predicted latent actions for continuous robot control. Our experiments compare CAC-VLA with existing VLA methods that rely on visual, linguistic, or action-level guidance, and further analyze the contribution of the proposed latent-action conditioning design through ablations on latent-action horizon and context gating. 

4.1 Experimental Setup Benchmarks and metrics. We evaluate CAC-VLA on LIBERO [13] and LIBERO-Plus [14]. LIBERO contains four multi-task manipulation suites, including Spatial, Object, Goal, and Long, while LIBERO-Plus evaluates robustness under distribution shifts in camera viewpoint, robot em-bodiment, language instruction, lighting, background, observation noise, and scene layout. We fol-low the official evaluation protocols and report success rate as the main metric, including per-suite results and the average score. 

Implementation. We implement CAC-VLA by extending the π0.5 architecture with VLM-side latent query tokens, an OAT-aligned latent-action prediction head, and context-gated expert condi-tioning. For consistency with recent action-guided VLA comparisons, we follow the benchmark protocol and baseline collection used in ACoT-VLA. 4.2 Simulation Experiments 

We compare CAC-VLA with existing VLA methods on LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus. These ex-periments evaluate whether VLM-predicted latent actions can provide effective action-structured conditioning for continuous expert control. 

Results on LIBERO. Tab. 1 reports the comparison on the standard LIBERO benchmark. CAC-VLA achieves an average success rate of 98.3%, with particularly strong results on Object and Goal. This demonstrates that the proposed latent-action interface can effectively support continuous expert control on standard manipulation tasks. 

Results on LIBERO-Plus. Tab. 2 reports results on LIBERO-Plus under the supervised fine-tuning setting. CAC-VLA achieves an average success rate of 89.5% across diverse distribution shifts, with strong performance under lighting, background, noise, and layout variations. These results suggest that latent-action conditioning remains useful under more challenging visual and environmental changes. 

4.3 Ablation Studies 

We conduct ablation studies to analyze two key design choices in CAC-VLA : the latent-action hori-zon and the context-gated conditioning mechanism. 

Effect of latent-action horizon. As shown in Tab. 3, Hl = 20 achieves the best average perfor-mance, suggesting that a moderate latent-action horizon provides the best trade-off between longer future-action context and relevance to the current action chunk. 

Effect of context gating. Tab. 4 ablates the latent-action conditioning design. Removing latent-action conditioning reduces the average success rate from 98.3% to 98.0%, while removing the context gate further lowers it to 97.9%. These results indicate that predicted latent actions are useful for expert conditioning, and that adaptive gated injection is more effective than fixed residual fusion. 4.4 Real-World Experiments 

To further evaluate CAC-VLA beyond simulation, we conduct an initial real-world experiment on a tabletop pick-and-place task, where a UR7e robot is instructed to pick up a block and place it into a target basket. We report Task Score for partial task progress and Full Success Rate (Full SR) for complete task success. Details about the setup, data collection, fine-tuning, evaluation protocol, and metric definitions are provided in App. C. As shown in Fig. 2, CAC-VLA achieves a Task Score of 72% and a Full SR of 64%, outperforming the π0.5 baseline by 24 and 48 percentage points, respectively. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We presented CAC-VLA , a Context-Gated Action Conditioning framework that equips VLA models with a VLM-native latent-action interface for continuous robot control. CAC-VLA trains dedicated VLM query tokens to predict structured latent actions and uses context-gated cross-attention to adaptively condition the action expert. Experiments on LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus demonstrate the effectiveness of latent-action conditioning, while ablations validate the importance of latent-action horizon and context-gated residual modulation. 

## 6 Limitations 

Although CAC-VLA shows promising simulation results and initial real-world validation, our real-world evaluation is limited to a single tabletop pick-and-place task, one robot embodiment, and a small number of demonstrations. The model may still fail when the predicted latent actions are inaccurate or when a fixed latent-action horizon does not match the current task phase, especially in longer-horizon or more diverse manipulation scenarios. This appendix provides additional implementation and training details for CAC-VLA . Unless other-wise specified, the same model architecture and optimization settings are used for both LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus. 

A.1 Model Configuration 

We implement CAC-VLA by modifying the π0.5 architecture. Specifically, we add learnable latent query tokens to the VLM, use a lightweight prediction head to regress raw OAT latent actions, and inject the resulting latent-action conditions into the action expert through context-gated cross-attention. The original π0.5 action expert is used as the continuous action generator, with a fixed expert horizon of He = 10 .For latent-action supervision, we use Nq = 8 latent query tokens and raw OAT latent dimension 

Dz = 4 . The latent-action horizon is set to Hl = 20 for LIBERO and Hl = 10 for LIBERO-Plus. The OAT tokenizer is kept frozen throughout VLA training. During training, it encodes ground-truth future action segments into raw latent-action targets. During inference, the OAT tokenizer is removed, and the action expert is conditioned only on latent actions predicted by the VLM query tokens. 

A.2 Optimization Details 

For both LIBERO and LIBERO-Plus, we train with a global batch size of 64 on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We use AdamW as the optimizer and clip the gradient norm to 1.0. We maintain an expo-nential moving average of model parameters with EMA decay 0.999. The learning rate follows a cosine schedule with 10K warmup steps. The peak learning rate is 

1.25 × 10 −5, the decay steps are set to 1M, and the final learning rate is also 1.25 × 10 −5. The latent alignment loss weight is set to λalign = 0 .1, and the latent-action conditioning dropout rate is set to 0.1. In the block-stacking task, the robot must grasp one block, move it above a target block, and place it stably on top. Both tasks use the same robot platform and sensing configuration. We use two Intel RealSense D435i RGB-D cameras for visual observation. One camera is mounted on the robot wrist to provide a local gripper-centric view, while the other is mounted above the workspace to provide a third-person view of the scene. The policy takes the two camera observations, the robot proprioceptive state, and a language instruction as input, and predicts continuous action chunks for robot execution. 

Data collection and fine-tuning. We collect 50 real-world demonstrations for each task. The demonstrations contain the complete manipulation procedure, including approaching the manipu-lated object, grasping it, transporting it toward the target, aligning it with the target, placing it, and releasing the gripper. The π0.5 baseline and CAC-VLA are fine-tuned separately for each task using exactly the same demonstrations and input modalities. For pick-and-place, both methods are fine-tuned with a learning rate of 1.25 × 10 −5. For block stacking, both methods are fine-tuned with a learning rate of 5 × 10 −5. Within each task, all training settings, including the learning rate, are identical between π0.5 and CAC-VLA to ensure a fair com-parison. The task-specific learning rates are fixed for both methods and are not tuned separately for 

CAC-VLA .

Evaluation protocol. Each method is evaluated over 25 independent trials per task. The two methods are evaluated using the same robot platform, camera setup, language instructions, object initialization protocol, and number of trials. For pick-and-place, we report Task Score and Full Success Rate (Full SR). A trial receives a Task Score of 0.5 if the robot successfully grasps the block but fails to place it inside the basket, and a score of 1.0 if the complete task succeeds. Otherwise, the trial receives a score of 0. The final Task Score is averaged over all trials. A trial is counted as a full success only when the block is released and remains stably inside the target basket at the end of execution. For block stacking, we report Stacking Success Rate (Stacking SR). A trial is counted as successful only when the manipulated block is released and remains stably supported on top of the target block at the end of execution. Failures include missing or dropping the manipulated block, inaccurate target alignment, displacing the target block, and producing an unstable stack. 15 Results. As shown in Fig. 2, CAC-VLA achieves a Task Score of 72% and a Full SR of 64% (16 /25 

successful trials) on pick-and-place, compared with 48% and 16% (4/25 successful trials) for the 

π0.5 baseline. On block stacking, CAC-VLA achieves a Stacking SR of 52% (13 /25 successful trials), compared with 36% (9/25 successful trials) for π0.5. These results indicate that context-gated latent-action conditioning improves execution reliability on both real-world tasks under the evaluated setting, with the largest improvement observed in complete pick-and-place success.
