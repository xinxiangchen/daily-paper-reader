# XS-VLA: Coupling Coarse-grained Spatial Distillation with Latent Flow Matching for Lightweight Robotic Control 

Lei Iok Tong 1, Qingchen Xie 1, Wei Huang 1, Ying Jie Yap 1, Yujie Zhang 2, Qianzhi Li 3, Xiaolong Liu *,Zhidong Deng 1,* 

Abstract — While Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have revolutionized multimodal understanding with strong spatial grounding, they remain computationally prohibitive for real-time robotic control. Conversely, lightweight models offer the efficiency required for edge deployment but suffer from “spatial blindness”—a lack or weakness of native spatial prediction capabilities. Furthermore, training Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models on mixed human demonstration data often degrades policy performance due to the complexity of modeling highly variant behaviors. These limitations severely hamper the utility of lightweight models in precise robotic manipulation. To bridge this gap, we propose XS-VLA, anovel two-stage framework. First, we distill spatial seman-tic knowledge from a capable teacher model (Qwen3-VL-4B) into the SmolVLM2-0.25B backbone. By fine-tuning on a curated dataset of coarse-grained spatial descriptions, we transform this lightweight model into a spatially grounded engine. Second, we leverage this enhanced backbone to con-dition a novel Latent Flow Matching policy. Unlike standard deterministic controllers, our policy couples a Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) with Flow Matching dynamics to robustly model complex, multimodal action distributions. Our model achieves new SOTA performance among models 

<0.5B on the Libero benchmark. Extensive experiments on the LIBERO benchmark demonstrate that this “distill-then-control” approach significantly enhances performance. XS-VLA achieves up to a 7.2% increase in success rates on average (23% increase on Libero-Long) compared to the SmolVLA 0.25B baseline and even outperforms the significantly larger 2.2B vanilla SmolVLA. Ultimately, in our ablation study, our framework demonstrates that specialized spatial tuning com-bined with generative latent flow control dramatically improves lightweight VLA performance, delivering a 3.2× speedup in mission execution compared to the previous lightweight flow matching policy.

However, a critical bottleneck remains in the deployment of these models: the trade-off between reasoning capability

LVLMs, with parameter counts often exceeding 7 billion (and up to hundreds of billions), typically operate at frequencies far below 1Hz on standard hardware, making them unsuitable for closed-loop low-level control. Conse-quently, the research community has turned its attention to lightweight VLMs that can run efficiently on edge devices like the NVIDIA Jetson Orin. While these lightweight models excel at inference speed and general semantic captioning (e.g., “there is a cup on the table”), they suffer from a significant deficiency: a lack of spatial grounding . Unlike their larger counterparts, small VLMs are rarely trained on massive datasets of spatial description. They tend to process images as “bags of con-cepts,” recognizing the presence of objects without precisely encoding their coordinates. For a robot, knowing that a cup exists is insufficient; it must know exactly where the cup is (x, y, z ) relative to its gripper to execute a successful grasp. The inability of vanilla lightweight models to understand relative spatial prepositions (e.g., “left of,” “behind”) leads to failure modes where the robot correctly identifies the target object but grasps at empty air nearby. In this paper, we address this “grounding gap” by propos-ing a cross-model distillation framework specifically opti-mized for the 0.25B parameter scale. We hypothesize that the limitation of small VLMs is not inherent to their ar-chitecture size but rather a consequence of their training data distribution. To test this, we leverage Qwen3-VL-4B, a model renowned for its ability to handle grounding tasks and output coordinate tokens, as a “teacher.” We construct a pipeline where Qwen3-VL-4B annotates a large-scale dataset of robotic manipulation scenes with coarse-grained spatial captions (Fig. 1, Fig. 2). We implement a two-stage training strategy. First, we fine-tune a 0.25B backbone on our synthetic spatial dataset. Second, we integrate this spatially-aware backbone into the VLA architecture. By inheriting these grounding-enhanced visual features while training the action projection layers,

Compounding the architectural challenge is the prohibitive cost of collecting high-quality, domain-specific robot data. To circumvent this data scarcity, a common paradigm is to co-train models on a mixture of limited robotic tra-jectories and abundant multimodal human demonstrations (e.g., human-centric videos and image-text pairs). However, effectively aligning the morphological differences between human demonstrations and robotic embodiments remains a persistent challenge. When forced into lightweight architec-tures, this mixed-data training often leads to catastrophic performance drops, forcing researchers into a rigid trade-off between model efficiency and task success rate. To address this challenge, we propose a novel end-to-end trainable hierarchical architecture combining a Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) [3] with a Flow Matching policy [4] to generate stable action trajectories. Our contributions are threefold: 1) Coarse-grained Spatial Distillation Pipeline: We develop an automated data generation pipeline us-ing Qwen3-VL-4B to synthesize high-quality coarse-grained spatial semantics labels specifically for robotic manipulation contexts and distill them into SmolVLM2 to be our backbone. 2) Latent Flow Matching VLA: We introduce a novel control architecture that integrates the grounded back-bone with a CVAE-based Flow Matching policy called XS-VLA. This allows the model to capture multimodal human demonstrations and generate stable, straight-line action trajectories. 3) SOTA among <0.5B VLAs and Efficiency: We val-idate that our 0.25B XS-VLA outperforms the current SOTA ( <0.5B), which is Vanilla SmolVLA (0.25B) baseline on the LIBERO benchmark (82.8% ↑ 90%). Furthermore, in ablation study, our method achieves a 3.2 × speedup compared to the previous lightweight flow matching policy, SmolVLA [5] architecture, in task completion. Qualitative analysis confirms that this efficiency does not come at the cost of performance; rather, the model generates smoother and more precise action trajectories, significantly improving overall ac-tion quality, such as, task execution speed and accuracy. 

II. Related Work 

A. Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) 

The landscape of computer vision has been reshaped by LVLMs trained on internet-scale image-text pairs. Models like Flamingo [6] introduced the paradigm of interleaving visual and textual data for few-shot learning. Following this, LLaVA [7] and InstructBLIP [8] demonstrated that visual instruction tuning could align image encoders (like CLIP [9]) with Large Language Models to follow complex user queries. More recently, models like Qwen3-VL-4B [2] introduced the concept of treating bounding box coordinates as special tokens, allowing for explicit grounding. 

B. Lightweight and Edge VLMs 

To address computational constraints, there is a grow-ing trend towards compact VLMs. SmolVLM2 [10], Mo-bileVLM [11], and TinyLLaVA [12] represent efforts to distill capabilities into architectures with fewer than 3 billion parameters. While efficient, these models often sacrifice performance on “hard” tasks like spatial reasoning. Our work specifically targets this limitation by injecting explicit spatial priors into the model and contributing an embodied AI. 

C. Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Models 

The concept of VLA models was popularized by RT-2 [13], which fine-tuned a Vision-Language Model (VLM) to output discretized robot actions directly as text tokens. Subsequently, open-source initiatives like OpenVLA [14] and Octo [15] democratized access to large-scale, generalist robotic policies. Recent advancements have further expanded VLA capabilities through enhanced reasoning and execution paradigms; for instance, ThinkAct [16] explicitly decouples high-level cognitive planning from low-level action genera-tion, while TraceVLA [17] leverages visual traces to improve fine-grained trajectory modeling. Conversely, SmolVLA [5] addresses the computational bottleneck of these larger net-works by scaling down the parameter count, facilitating real-time control without catastrophic degradation in task success rates. Despite these strides, existing VLAs often struggle with precise spatial reasoning. As noted in SpatialVLM [18], standard architectures frequently fail to distinguish between spatially adjacent objects. To address this gap, recent models like SpatialVLA [19] and FPC-VLA [20] have explored enhanced spatial and point-cloud representations to improve manipulation accuracy. Building upon these insights, our work tackles this limitation head-on by integrating explicit coarse-grained spatial grounding directly into the visual encoder, ensuring robust and precise spatial awareness for continuous robot control. 

D. Generative Policies in Robotics 

Traditional behavior cloning often suffers from the “mul-timodal” problem (averaging conflicting actions). Diffusion Policies [21] addressed this by modeling the action dis-tribution as a denoising process. Recently, flow matching policy [22], [4] has emerged as a more efficient alternative, providing deterministic inference paths. Our work integrates the CVAE concept from ACT [23] with the superior training stability of flow matching, tailored for a VLA architecture. 

E. Knowledge Distillation in Multimodal Systems 

Knowledge distillation has emerged as a critical technique for transferring the capabilities of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) into lightweight, deployable architectures [24], [25]. While traditional distillation often relies on logit matching, recent works emphasize the transfer of structured, task-specific knowledge, such as spatial reasoning [18], [25]. In this work, we apply symbolic distillation specifically to the spatial domain. By leveraging a powerful teacher model, Qwen3-VL-4B [2], to generate precise keypoint an-notations, we bypass the prohibitive costs associated with human annotation of robotic datasets. This automated dataset generation paradigm allows us to significantly scale up the spatial training data for our 0.25B parameter model. Consequently, we effectively “download” the teacher model’s robust spatial understanding directly into the smaller model’s weights, ensuring high-fidelity spatial awareness without the computational overhead of an LVLM. 

III. Method 

Our XS-VLA framework is composed of three distinct stages: (1) Spatial Data Generation via Teacher Distillation, (2) Spatial Instruction Tuning of the Student VLM, and (3) Integration into the Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Policy via Latent Flow Matching. 

A. Stage 1: Spatial Semantic Generation via Qwen 

The core premise of our method is that the student model (0.25B) lacks exposure to grounding data. To remedy this, we adopt Qwen3-VL-4B as a teacher and design a two-step spatial description generation pipeline (Fig. 1) using simulated LIBERO [26] images Draw = {Ii}Ni=1 .In the first step, Qwen3-VL-4B predicts two grasp key-points in integer pixel coordinates from each image using instruction-based prompts. The predicted keypoints are over-laid on the original image to form an annotated image ˜Ii.In the second step, the annotated image ˜Ii and an ad-ditional prompt are fed into Qwen3-VL-4B to generate a fine-grained spatial description Ti, expressed using a pre-defined set of nine discrete directional regions, namely top, top-left, top-right, center, center-left, center-right, bottom, bottom-left, and bottom-right (Fig. 2). This process produces training tuples ( ˜Ii, T i), enabling the scalable construction of grounded spatial supervision for effective knowledge distil-lation on our backbone. 

B. Stage 2: Fine-tuning the 0.25B Backbone 

We initialize the student model with its pre-trained weights (SigLIP vision encoder + SmolLM [27] text decoder), and fine-tune it on the generated tuples (Pre-training part in Fig. 3). Instead of regressing continuous bounding-box co-ordinates, we formulate localization as a direction-oriented discrete prediction task, enabling robust and compact coarse-grained spatial grounding. C. Stage 3: Latent Flow Matching Policy Integration 

To transform our pre-trained VLM into a control policy, we discard the language head and integrate a conditional action expert vθ . Unlike standard deterministic policies, we propose a fully end-to-end trainable hierarchical architecture combining a Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) from ACT [23] with a flow matching policy [4] (Fig. 3). During training, the entire pipeline, including the vision encoder, language backbone, action expert, and CVAE, is optimized jointly to predict an action chunk At = at:t+k

where k is the chunk size. 

1) Latent Intent Modeling (CVAE): Human demonstra-tions often exhibit multimodality (e.g., different speeds or approach angles). To model this, we employ a Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) [3]. The CVAE encoder, denoted as Eϕ, is implemented as a BERT-like Trans-former [28]. It processes a sequence comprising a [CLS] 

token, which consists of learned weights that are randomly initialized, the proprioceptive state ct, and the ground-truth action chunk At. The output corresponding to the [CLS] 

token is then projected to parameterize a diagonal Gaussian latent distribution, 

qϕ(z|At, ct).

Input Staging: To prevent the VAE from being directly driven by the flow matching loss, the proprioceptive state and target actions are first detached from the computation graph. They are linearly projected into a shared embedding space of dimension dmodel = 256 . A [CLS] token is prepended to this sequence. After adding fixed sinusoidal positional encodings, the combined sequence is transposed to a time-major format to yield the final encoder input. 

Encoder Core: The sequence is processed by aTransformer encoder. Each layer employs a robust pre-normalization architecture, applying LayerNorm strictly be-fore both the Multi-Head Self-Attention and the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) blocks, followed by residual con-nections and dropout. 

Latent Head and Reparameterization: To extract the latent intent, we isolate the final hidden state corresponding to the [CLS] token. A linear projection head maps this state to the parameters of a diagonal Gaussian, outputting the mean μ and log-variance log σ2. A latent intent vector 

z is then sampled via the reparameterization trick: 

z = μ + exp(log σ2/2) ⊙ ϵ, ϵ ∼ N (0, I).

This latent z encapsulates the high-level “style” of the motion. During inference, the VAE encoder is bypassed, and

2) Architecture: Interleaved Attention: Following the ar-chitecture proposed in SmolVLA [5], the action expert vθ

is implemented as a conditional flow matching Transformer. To effectively fuse the multimodal context with the action generation process, we adopt an interleaved attention strategy consisting of two distinct layer types: 

Cross-Attention Layers (Primary Modality): In the majority of the layers, information flows unidirectionally from the prefix to the action expert suffix. The prefix stream, containing images and text embeddings from the VLM, the proprioceptive state projected by an MLP, and the projected latent z from the VAE, first undergoes self-attention. The expert suffix stream, containing noisy action tokens aτ and flow time embeddings τ , then acts as the query ( Q), cross-attending to the keys ( K) and values ( V) of the prefix. This allows the action tokens to continuously pull rich multimodal context before being mapped to the target velocity u.

Periodic Joint Self-Attention Layers: To prevent the action representations from completely decoupling from the VLM backbone, we interleave joint self-attention layers every N layers. In these layers, the prefix and suffix sequences are concatenated into a single stream. Subject to causal masking, this allows for symmetric interaction where the action tokens and prefix tokens jointly refine a shared representation.

IV. Experiments 

A. Experimental Setup 

Environment: We evaluate our method using LIBERO [26], a comprehensive benchmark for lifelong robot learning based on the MuJoCo physics engine [29]. We train our XS-VLA model on the same Lerobot-Libero dataset as SmolVLA [5] used for 160,000 steps. 

Task Suites: We evaluate on four distinct suites: 1) LIBERO-Spatial: Tasks that require reasoning about spatial relations. 2) LIBERO-Object: tasks centered on manipulating dif-ferent objects. 3) LIBERO-Goal: goal-conditioned tasks where the robot must adapt to changing targets. 4) LIBERO-Long: 10 long-horizon tasks from the LIBERO-100 collection. 

Baselines: Our evaluation includes external models such as Diffusion Policy [21], Octo (0.1B) [15], OpenVLA (7B) [14], and several recent 4B–7B VLA architectures (SpatialVLA [19], TraceVLA [17], ThinkAct [16], FPC-VLA [20]). To isolate the architectural improvements of our method, we perform direct comparisons against Vanilla SmolVLA [5] at three scales (0.25B, 0.5B, and 2.25B). We report the performance of Diffusion Policy and Octo as evaluated in the SmolVLA paper [5]. Additionally, we compare our XS-VLA to Dita [30], a recent lightweight model. The results for all other baselines are sourced from their respective original publications. Finally, to disentangle the effects of spatial pretraining, we introduce SmolVLA-PD-0.25B, a baseline that utilizes our SmolVLM2-PD-0.25B while strictly maintaining the SmolVLA architecture.

C. Real-World Evaluation on Xlerobot Platform 

To evaluate our model’s performance in a physical envi-ronment, we designed a bimanual carrot transfer task on the Xlerobot [31] platform. The task requires the robot to grasp a carrot with its left hand, transfer it to its right hand, and place it in a designated target location. To rigorously test the model’s ability to handle multimodal action distributions, we curated a dataset of 100 demonstra-tions collected via teleoperation by three distinct expert oper-ators. This multi-expert data introduces significant variability in execution style, including differences in approach angles, transfer timings, and motion speeds. We train ACT [23] for 80,000 steps, SmolVLA (0.5B) [5] for 60,000 steps, and our XS-VLA for 20,000 steps. We compare our XS-VLA against these two models in the exact same environment setup. We employ a partial credit scoring system: successfully picking up the carrot yields 0.5 points, and successfully transferring and placing it yields an additional 0.5 points (max 1.0 per trial). Performance (Table II) was measured over 10 trials. XS-VLA achieves the highest performance with a total score of 

7.5 . It outperforms both ACT (7.0) and Vanilla SmolVLA (6.5). We attribute this superiority to our Latent Flow Match-ing mechanism: while the vanilla model struggles to average the conflicting strategies of the three experts (often resulting in jitter or mean-seeking behavior), our CVAE component successfully encodes these distinct behavioral modes into the latent space z, allowing the policy to conditionally generate coherent trajectories consistent with a single expert’s style. VI. Discussion and Limitations 

Our evaluation demonstrates that lightweight 0.25B VLMs can achieve competitive performance on spatial manipulation tasks without the massive computational cost of 7B+ parame-ter models. However, limitations persist. Our model still faces difficulties in the goal-conditioned tasks where unique object geometries are required, and reliance on a 2D teacher means the model sometimes struggles with depth ambiguity. VII. Conclusion 

In this work, we introduce XS-VLA , a highly efficient 0.25B Vision-Language-Action model developed through a novel two-stage framework. By first distilling coarse-grained spatial grounding capabilities from Qwen3-VL-4B into a compact backbone, and subsequently coupling it with arobust Latent Flow Matching policy, we achieve state-of-the-art performance among models ( <0.5B) at a fraction of the computational cost. Our findings demonstrate that with the appropriate architectural design and training curricu-lum, lightweight models can effectively overcome the spatial blindness” and control brittleness” typically associated with smaller parameter scales.
