Abstract: Pretrained generative robot policies based on flow matching and diffu-sion have achieved impressive results across a wide range of manipulation tasks. Yet real-world deployments routinely expose failure modes outside the pretraining distribution. Closing these gaps typically requires large-scale data collection or online reinforcement learning on physical hardware, which is impractical for rapid and safe adaptation. We present FlowDAgger, a sample- and compute-efficient method for adapting frozen generative robot policies from human interventions in latent space. Our key idea is action inversion: each human expert action is mapped to the noise that would have produced it under the frozen base policy, using reverse-time integration followed by local refinement. The resulting in-verted noise provides supervision for a lightweight latent policy that steers the base model at deployment time, enabling rapid skill acquisition while preserving its behavioral priors. We evaluate FlowDAgger in simulation and on real-world bi-manual and single-arm manipulation, adapting both action-head VLAs and world-action models from a handful of interventions. FlowDAgger outperforms super-vised fine-tuning and latent-space RL baselines and preserves pretrained skills on held-out tasks, offering a practical path for adapting robot foundation models in the real world. # 1 Introduction 

Foundation models for robot manipulation have achieved impressive results on a wide range of tasks by leveraging large-scale demonstration datasets and expressive generative architectures. The dominant approach across modern systems is a learned generative process that maps random noise to actions conditioned on observations. Building on the visuomotor diffusion policy [1], this approach underlies recent vision-language-action (VLA) models [2, 3], world-action models (WAM) [4, 5, 6], and large multitask diffusion policies [7, 8]. Trained on large, diverse demonstration corpora, these policies encode broad behavioral priors that transfer surprisingly well across embodiments and tasks. However, in any specific deployment they routinely fail due to unfamiliar objects, novel scene dynamics, embodiment quirks, and long-tail edge cases not covered by the pretraining mixture. Existing approaches for closing these gaps are problematic. Collecting additional demonstrations to cover the state-action space for subsequent offline finetuning is tedious; finetuning large-scale mod-els is computationally expensive; moreover, the resulting policy tends to erode broader skills already present in the base model. Interactive imitation in the action space [9, 10, 11] handles the covariate-shift problem, but it, too, works by adapting the entire generative policy, an expensive and unstable update that can corrupt the learned prior; residual methods [12, 13] keep the base frozen and learn arXiv:2607.08877v1 [cs.RO] 9 Jul 2026 Figure 1: An overview of F LOW DA GGER . These failures suggest that the adaptation interface matters: weight-space updates can damage the pretrained prior, while unconstrained action-space corrections can leave the support of the generative policy. Latent-space reinforcement learning, ex-emplified by DSRL [14], instead trains a lightweight controller in the policy’s noise space. This is markedly more sample-efficient, but relies on a reward signal and autonomous exploration, both of which are bottlenecks for real-world deployment. In this work, we adapt generative robot policies from human corrections by learning in the policy’s latent space. Prior latent-space methods show that a frozen generative policy can be steered effec-tively through its noise variables, but human experts intervene by providing corrective actions, not noise targets. Key to our approach is action inversion – a mechanism for mapping a human expert’s intervention actions into corresponding latent-space vectors via reverse-time integration and local refinement. Given an observation s and an expert action a∗, we invert a∗ to a noise vector w∗ such that the base policy’s generative process from w∗ at s reproduces a∗. Each (s, w ∗) pair becomes supervision for a small latent policy that operates within the generative process of the frozen base policy. We never modify the base policy’s weights, relying only on forward passes through the base model and adapting it entirely through its input noise. We refer to this approach as FlowDAgger , and instantiate it for adapting flow-matching and diffusion-based policies in the real world. We evaluate FlowDAgger in simulation and on physical bimanual and single-arm manipulation, adapting both action-head VLAs and world-action models. Across these settings, FlowDAgger reaches target success rates with substantially fewer human in-terventions than offline fine-tuning via behavior cloning and action-space DAgger, and with fewer environment interactions than latent-space reinforcement learning. Because the base policy is never modified, FlowDAgger preserves pretrained skills on held-out tasks, and adaptation remains light enough to train on a consumer GPU. 

# 2 Related Work 

Generative robot policies. Diffusion Policy [1] showed that diffusion-based action heads can cap-ture multi-modal manipulation behaviors directly from demonstrations, and the approach has since been scaled to large multitask settings [7, 8, 15]. Flow matching [16], a closely related approach, underlies recent VLAs [2, 3] and WAMs [4, 5, 17, 6]. FlowDAgger targets this generative subfamily: any policy whose action generation is the integration of an ODE driven by a noise sample. 

Interactive imitation and human-in-the-loop learning. DAgger [9] introduced on-policy correc-tive supervision to address covariate shift in behavioral cloning, and HG-DAgger [10] and IWR [11] made human-gated variants practical for manipulation. Sirius [18] and Sirius-Fleet [19] extend this paradigm with shared-autonomy deployment and trust-weighted behavior cloning, scaling human-2in-the-loop learning to robot fleets. HIL-SERL [20] and RLIF [21] fold interventions into an RL loop instead, combining human corrections with a reward signal to drive policy improvement. Building on residual policy learning [22, 23], residual DAgger [12, 13] keeps the base policy frozen and learns an additive action-space correction from human interventions. FlowDAgger shares the intervention-driven data collection of these methods but supervises in the policy’s noise space. 

Latent and noise-space policy learning. DSRL [14] is the closest methodological neighbor to our approach. DSRL trains a small controller in the noise space of a frozen diffusion policy using RL, and demonstrates substantial sample efficiency relative to weight-adaptation fine-tuning of the base policy. FlowDAgger shares DSRL’s architectural premise – a lightweight policy acting in the noise input of a frozen generative base – and differs in the source of supervision: DSRL learns from autonomous rollouts under a reward function, whereas FlowDAgger learns from human corrections inverted back into noise space. Latent-action methods more broadly [24] compress demonstrations into a learned action space; we instead operate in the noise space the base policy already exposes. 

Inversion in generative models. Inverting a generated sample back to its initial noise is a well-studied operation in image synthesis. DDIM inversion [25] recovers the deterministic latent for a diffusion sample; null-text inversion [26] improves fidelity under classifier-free guidance; and analo-gous techniques have been developed for rectified-flow models [27]. FlowDAgger brings inversion-based adaptation to both flow-matching action heads and WAMs. This requires two modifications of the standard inversion machinery. For flow-matching action heads, we use a per-step fixed-point variant suited to their few-step ODE schedules, where the trajectory-level methods standard in im-age diffusion become unstable (Section 4.1). For WAMs, where no action-only process exists, we invert the joint world-action diffusion directly, recovering a joint-latent noise from a target in which only the action frame carries the correction (Section 4.2). 

# 3 Background 

Generative action policies. We consider a pretrained generative policy πgp : S → P (A) that maps an observation s ∈ S to a distribution over an action space A, where a ∈ A is an action chunk of horizon H. We use “observation” for the policy’s full input, which may include a history of past sensor readings. Given s, the policy draws a noise sample w ∼ N (0 , I ) from the noise space W

and transforms it into a by integrating an ordinary differential equation (ODE) defined by a learned velocity field vθ :

dx dt = vθ (x, t, s ), x(0) = w, a = x(1) . (1) We write πgp (s, w ) for the resulting noise-to-action map, a = πgp (s, w ), and πgp (a | s) for the action distribution it induces when w ∼ N (0 , I ). Both flow-matching [16] and diffusion policies [1] fit this description: the former learns the velocity field vθ directly, while the latter’s reverse process admits an equivalent probability-flow ODE. FlowDAgger applies to any base policy of this form. 

Euler discretization. In practice, the map is evaluated with a K-step Euler discretization of step size ∆t = 1 /K . With x0 = w and tk = k ∆t, the action is produced by the recurrence 

xk+1 = xk + ∆ t v θ (xk, t k, s ), k = 0 , . . . , K − 1, a = xK . (2) Flow-matching action heads typically use a small number of steps ( K on the order of 10), in contrast to the long schedules ( K in the 100s) common in image diffusion. This few-step regime is central to the inversion procedure in Section 4.1. 

Noise as the adaptation surface. For a fixed observation s, the base policy is a deterministic map 

πgp (s, ·) from noise to action, so the noise sample w alone determines the action. The action distri-bution is therefore controlled entirely by the distribution over W. This exposes the noise space as a surface for adapting the policy without modifying θ: replacing the standard draw w ∼ N (0 , I ) with a state-conditioned choice of w steers the policy’s behavior while leaving the pretrained generative process intact [14]. 3Human-in-the-loop adaptation. We consider the standard interactive setting, where a human may intervene in behavior induced by a pretrained policy. At each step, the policy proposes an action; when the behavior is unsatisfactory, the expert takes over and provides a corrective action a∗ for the current observation s. This yields correction pairs (s, a ∗) on states the deployed policy actually visits, as in DAgger [9]. Our goal is to use these corrections to adapt the policy efficiently – without modifying the base policy’s weights or using a reward signal. 

# 4 Method 

FlowDAgger has two components: (1) action inversion (Section 4.1) converts a corrective action into a target in the base policy’s noise space and (2) a small noise policy (Section 4.3) is trained on these targets to steer the frozen base policy at deployment. Section 4.2 extends action inversion to WAMs, whose action generation is entangled with future-state prediction. 

4.1 Action Inversion 

Latent-space adaptation methods typically rely on rewards or supervision already expressed in the policy’s latent space. Human interventions, however, arrive as corrective actions. Action inversion converts these action-space corrections into noise-space supervision: given an observation s and corrective action a∗, it recovers a noise vector w∗ such that the frozen generative policy reproduces the correction, πgp (s, w ∗) ≈ a∗; the pair (s, w ∗) is then a supervision target for the noise policy. The map πgp (s, ·) composes K Euler steps (Section 3) and has no closed-form inverse, so we invert it one step at a time. Each forward step xk+1 = xk + ∆ t v θ (xk, t k, s ) rearranges to the implicit equation xk = xk+1 − ∆t v θ (xk, t k, s ), which we solve by fixed-point iteration:

Inverting one correction costs K M evaluations of vθ , requires no backpropagation through the base policy, and runs online as corrections are collected. 

4.2 Inverting World-Action Models 

The inversion of Section 4.1 assumes a base policy whose action generation is a self-contained process in action space. WAMs break this assumption: they generate an action chunk jointly with future world states, as slices of a single latent video sequence [4, 5], so no action-only process exists to invert. FlowDAgger nonetheless applies to this family, by inverting the joint generative process directly. We describe the procedure for Cosmos-Policy [4], a WAM built on a pretrained video diffusion model.

Adaptation loop. FlowDAgger collects corrections on-policy, in the DAgger fashion [9]. The current policy is deployed with a human operator in the loop; when the operator judges its behavior unsatisfactory, they intervene with a corrective action a∗, producing a correction pair (s, a ∗) on a state the deployed policy actually visited. Action inversion (Section 4.1) converts each correction into a noise-space target (s, w ∗), which is added to a training set D. The noise policy is trained to regress these targets by minimizing 

L(ϕ) = E(s,w ∗)∼D πw(s) − w∗ 22. (5) Deployment, correction, inversion, and training are interleaved: D is aggregated across rounds, so later corrections target the states induced by the adapted policy. Corrections are sparse relative to the policy’s own transitions, so regressing πw on corrections alone overfits the handful of intervened states. FlowDAgger therefore structures D as two buffers: inverted corrections (s, w ∗) fill an inter-vention buffer, while the noise that produced each transition of the policy’s successful autonomous rollouts fills a second buffer. Every training batch draws in equal proportion from the two. The autonomous buffer keeps πw anchored to the base policy’s behavior on states it already handles, while the fixed share keeps the sparse corrections prominent in every update. 

# 5 Experiments 

Our evaluation is designed to answer four questions: (i) how sample- and compute-efficient is FlowDAgger relative to existing adaptation methods; (ii) does noise-space adaptation transfer across base-policy families, including world-action models with no separable action head; (iii) does it pre-serve and leverage the base policy’s behavioral prior better than fine-tuning; and (iv) does it deliver these gains on real hardware across a range of manipulation tasks. 

5.1 Setup Tasks and platforms. We evaluate on a range of manipulation tasks in simulation and on real hardware, from basic pick-and-place to contact-rich and bimanual manipulation. In simulation we use MetaWorld [28], adapting on a diverse set of contact-rich tasks; the tasks evaluated per base policy are listed in Tables 1 and 2. On real hardware we use two bimanual platforms, a FR3 Duo 

(two FR3 arms) and a Dual UR5e setup; depending on the task the policy controls a single arm or both. The unimanual tasks are Block Pick, Glassware Stacking, and three tasks from the BusyBox manipulation benchmark [29] (Button Push, Slider, Wire Pull); the bimanual tasks are Jenga Stack, Toolbox Packing, and Plug Insertion. The action- and weight-space DAgger baselines consume the same correction stream but gain far less, so the advantage comes from where the correction is applied, not from the corrections alone. # 6 Limitations 

Like other latent-space adaptation methods, FlowDAgger adapts behavior by steering a frozen gen-erative policy through its latent variables rather than modifying the policy itself. Its adaptation capacity is therefore bounded by the support of the pretrained model: if a desired behavior lies far outside the policy’s action manifold, inversion can only recover the closest behavior representable by the base policy. In such cases, additional data or weight-space adaptation may be needed. Adaptation also depends on the quality and coverage of human interventions. As with other DAgger-style methods, FlowDAgger can only improve behavior in regions of the state space where correc-tive supervision is provided, and systematic biases or inconsistencies in those corrections may be reflected in the adapted policy. Finally, inversion accuracy can degrade for highly multimodal ac-tion distributions or poorly conditioned generative dynamics, reducing the fidelity with which expert corrections are represented in latent space. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We introduced FlowDAgger, a framework for adapting pretrained generative robot policies from human interventions in latent space. Action inversion maps corrective actions to the latent variables that would have produced them, providing supervision for a lightweight noise policy while keeping the foundation model frozen. Across simulation, real robots, action-head VLAs, diffusion poli-cies, and world-action models, FlowDAgger improves from only a handful of interventions while preserving pretrained capabilities. More broadly, FlowDAgger treats interaction as a way to supervise the latent decisions of a frozen generative policy. Instead of modifying a foundation model’s parameters to absorb new experience, we recover the latent decisions that would have produced expert behavior and learn to reproduce them directly. This perspective decouples adaptation from model scale, preserves pretrained capa-bilities, and gives a practical mechanism for improving robot foundation models through interaction while keeping them stable, reusable, and computationally efficient. 8References

This appendix expands the world-action model inverter sketched in Section 4.2. Appendix A.1 gives the full EDM-schedule inversion procedure used for Cosmos-Policy; Appendix A.2 documents an action-frame-only variant that an earlier version of the inverter optimized and that the joint-process inversion of Section 4.2 replaces; Appendix A.3 describes how the noise policy is parameterized for the resulting high-dimensional joint noise. 

A.1 Inverting the EDM diffusion schedule 

Cosmos-Policy generates with an EDM-style diffusion process rather than a flow-matching ODE, which changes the recurrence inversion must undo. The noise schedule does not terminate at zero noise: it runs from a large σmax down to a nonzero σmin (here σmax = 80 , σmin = 4 ), and the forward sampler we invert is a 1-Euler integrator over σmax → σmin followed by a single terminal denoise D(·, σ min ). The 1-Euler integration is inverted with the same per-step fixed-point iteration as in Section 4.1, applied to the reverse of the EDM update; because each step depends only on the current latent and noise level, the reversal is memoryless and gradient-free. The terminal denoise at σmin is a strong transform – the schedule does not bottom out near identity – so a single explicit reverse step cannot invert it. We recover its input with a short local Adam solve anchored at the base policy’s own pre-terminal state, backpropagating through one denoiser call per Adam step. This terminal solve is the only part of the inversion that backpropagates through the base model; like the rest of the inverter it requires no backprop through the chained sampler and runs online as corrections are collected. 

A.2 Why action-frame-only inversion is insufficient 

An earlier variant of the inverter optimized only the action-frame noise (112 dimensions in our configuration) with Adam, held the non-action frames at a fixed reference, and minimized the action-reconstruction loss through the chained denoiser calls of the forward sampler. This consistently floored at a non-trivial reconstruction error – about 0.04 even when inverting the base policy’s own actions and 0.076 on real expert actions – and grew worse with more denoising steps, because the chained-denoiser objective is sharply non-convex in the action-frame noise alone. Inverting the joint process instead replaces that hard optimization with the exact per-step fixed-point inverse described in Section 4.2 and a single small autograd-based solve at σmin .

A.3 Parameterizing the noise policy for world-action models 

For action-head base policies the noise is action-chunk shaped and low-dimensional enough that the noise policy πw regresses it directly. Because πw is trained by supervised regression on inverted-noise targets rather than by reinforcement learning, it tolerates this dimensionality without the re-ductions that noise-space RL requires: DSRL [14], for instance, collapses π0’s 1,600 -dimensional chunk noise to a single per-step vector repeated across the chunk to keep SAC stable. World-action models are different. The joint noise returned by the inverter is of order 10 5 dimen-sions, too high for πw to regress directly even under supervision. We compared two options for πw’s output. Full : regress the entire joint latent – maximum capacity but no prior. Basis : regress k=64 

PCA coefficients fit on inverted-noise targets collected during a brief base-policy warmup, anchoring the output to the manifold of successful base-policy noises. Both worked; the basis parameterization was somewhat more stable and is the default in our Cosmos-Policy experiments. Either choice is downstream of inversion, which always operates on the full joint process.

B.1 Inversion procedure comparison 

We compare the per-step fixed-point inversion of Section 4.1 against (i) a single explicit Eu-ler reverse pass, (ii) trajectory-level fixed-point inversion, which corrects the global trajectory-reconstruction residual [27], and (iii) optimization-based inversion (Adam on an action-reconstruction loss backpropagated through the frozen decoder). All variants invert the same scripted-expert chunks on the assembly task and reconstruct through the frozen π0.5 decoder with the standard K=10 -step Euler schedule (Table 5). Per-step fixed-point inversion is roughly an order of magnitude more accurate than every trajectory-level alternative – at M =5 its Action MSE is ∼ 20 × lower than Euler reverse and ∼ 14 × lower than trajectory-level FP – and ∼ 3× faster per chunk. Cranking the baselines does not close the gap: trajectory FP saturates by k=5 and a 20-step Adam refinement only matches a single Euler pass (Appendix B.2). Both correct a global residual they cannot attribute to any single step, and at
