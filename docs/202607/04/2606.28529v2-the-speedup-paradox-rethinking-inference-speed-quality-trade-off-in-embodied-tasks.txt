Figure 1: Overview of the speed-quality trade-off in embodied inference. Unlike static ML tasks, where stronger lossy optimization monotonically trades accuracy for per-step speed, lightweight-ing embodied policies yields non-monotonic task-level behavior: both end-to-end completion time (static tasks) and task success rate (dynamic tasks) may exhibit a sweet spot, whose location further shifts with the deployment hardware. 

Abstract: Embodied foundation models have recently been widely used to im-prove robot generalization and task success rates. Previous works apply lossy efficient-inference techniques such as quantization, pruning, and asynchronous inference, accepting small action quality degradation in exchange for lower per-step computation cost and inter-action latency. However, unlike traditional static ML tasks, embodied tasks involve repeated interaction with the environment, and task-level performance is determined not only by per-step cost, but also by closed-loop effects unique to embodied execution, which remain insufficiently charac-terized in current efficient-inference studies. In this work, we propose TISED (Task-level Inference Speedup Effect Decomposition), an analytical framework that unifies diverse lossy inference optimization techniques and decomposes their effects on static and dynamic tasks, and uncovers some paradoxical effects on task-level performance: (1) on static tasks , optimization sometimes can lengthen end-to-end per-task completion time even as per-step latency drops; (2) on dy-namic tasks , moderate lossy optimization can raise task success rate even above the baseline; and (3) the monotonicity and sweet-spot location of both effects can shift with hardware configuration. Together, our findings provide a new perspec-tive on adapting inference optimization techniques to embodied tasks.

1 Introduction 

Embodied models such as Vision-Language-Action models (VLA) [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8] and World-Action Models (WAM) [9, 10, 11, 12] have been widely deployed across diverse embodied sce-narios. These models are typically obtained by fine-tuning large-scale pretrained vision-language models [13, 14] and world models [15, 16], yielding strong cross-task generalization and improved robustness to environmental variation. However, to fully accommodate these capabilities, they usu-ally have large parameter counts and compute FLOPs at inference time, posing a key barrier to edge deployment and real-time control. To address this, many recent works apply lossy inference optimization techniques to embodied mod-els, such as quantization [17, 18], cache reuse [19, 20, 21], sampling step reduction [22, 23, 24], and asynchronous scheduling [25, 26, 27]. These methods have been shown to reduce per-step inference latency or shorten the gap between consecutive action chunks with slight accuracy degradation. Em-bodied tasks, however, differ fundamentally: Each cycle of the inference-execution loop consists of one policy inference and the subsequent action execution, and the loop runs for many such cycles be-fore a rollout terminates. The per-step latency reductions from these methods therefore capture only a fragment of task-level performance: they ignore how many cycles the rollout ultimately requires until success, how the environment evolves during model inference, and how all of these interact with hardware performance across inference and execution costs. To analyze how lossy inference optimization shapes embodied task performance beyond per-step latency, we propose TISED (Task-level Inference Speedup Effect Decomposition) analysis in Fig-ure 1. First, we place policy-intrinsic and execution-aware optimization on a common per-chunk time formula. Building on this unified view, we model the end-to-end objective separately for the two task regimes: on static tasks, end-to-end completion time is jointly determined by the per-chunk inference time and the number of chunks that the full rollout requires; on dynamic tasks, task success rate is jointly determined by action quality and observation staleness. We empirically test these predictions with a progressive-lightweighting sweep across task types, model backbones, optimization methods, and compute platforms, from which three observations emerge: (1) On static tasks, end-to-end completion time is sometimes non-monotonic in the lightweighting strength due to the deterioration of action quality (per-step inference latency reduc-tion ̸ = end-to-end per-task latency reduction). (2) On dynamic tasks, task success rate is sometimes 

non-monotonic in the lossy optimization strength due to the trade-off between degraded action qual-ity and reduced observation staleness (static task accuracy ̸ = dynamic task accuracy). (3) Fixing the task and lightweighting method, the above trends and the location of the sweet spot shifts with the hardware compute budget. Together, these three observations show that the per-step speed-quality trade-off familiar from static ML no longer predicts task-level outcomes in embodied closed-loop execution—speed and quality interact through rollout length, action delay, and hardware budget. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 Embodied Foundation Models 

Recent progress in vision-language-action models (VLA) [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8] and world-action models (WAM) [9, 10, 11, 12] have enabled stronger open-world generalization, task adaptation, and large-scale policy transfer. However, these capabilities come at a high inference cost. For example, by default π0 [3] (3.3B parameters) is deployed on high-end consumer GPUs such as RTX 4090, and Cosmos-Policy [9] (2B parameters) on datacenter-class accelerators such as H100. This is a key barrier to edge deployment and low-latency control. In this work, rather than scaling capabilities or enhancing generalization, we study how to deploy these models efficiently in the inference-execution loop. 22.2 Lossy Optimizations for Embodied Model Inference 

To reduce the computational burden of embodied models, recent work has explored a range of lossy lightweight inference strategies. These methods can be grouped into two categories: (1) Policy-intrinsic optimization , which reduces the cost of each forward pass through techniques such as quan-tization [17, 18, 28, 29], denoise step reduction [30, 31, 32, 33], and cache reuse [19, 20, 21, 34]. For example, Q-VLA [18] analyzes how different components of a VLA vary in quantization sensitivity and assigns mixed precision accordingly to minimize the resulting action error; VLA-Cache [19] ex-ploits the sparsity of background visual information across perception steps and reuses less-salient tokens to reduce redundant computation. (2) Execution-aware optimization , which improves ef-fective control frequency by overlapping inference with execution, such as asynchronous schedul-ing [25, 26, 27]. For example, VLASH [25] rolls the robot state forward using the previously gen-erated action chunk to predict the state at execution time, and further reduces prediction-execution misalignment by fine-tuning. Most of these works focus only on reducing the latency of a single inference, leaving a systematic analysis of end-to-end effects in embodied scenarios largely unex-plored. In this work, rather than targeting a specific lightweight algorithm, we provide a unified modeling framework to analyze the unique impact of such algorithms on embodied tasks. 

2.3 Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Characterizations 

Aggressive lossy optimization methods can achieve significant gains in latency or energy efficiency while harming the accuracy. Previous works such as Deep Compression [35] established a trade-off between efficiency and accuracy in deep learning models. Subsequently, a lot of research has attempted to push this Pareto frontier forward in vision and language models [36, 37, 38, 39, 40]. In embodied systems, this relationship becomes more complex due to error accumulation and closed-loop feedback. Works such as [41, 42] introduced a new trade-off between efficiency and accuracy in time-sensitive tasks, but their scope was limited to object detection or LLM agents. Works such as [34, 43, 44] have characterized the inference performance of VLA models across various hardware devices but lack task-level performance. Work [45] provides valuable analysis of the negative impact of model lightweighting on execution in embodied task scenarios, while their focus is on the negative side. In this work, we further explore the synergy between the positive and negative effects of lightweight optimization and conduct a cross-hardware study. 

3 Task-Level Inference Speedup Effect Decomposition 

Embodied inference optimization operates within an inference-execution loop, not during a sin-gle forward pass. Therefore, a per-step speedup does not translate directly into a task-level gain: it changes both how many control cycles a rollout needs and how stale the observation is when the resulting action is executed. These two effects give rise to different failure modes in differ-ent task regimes. We develop this view into TISED, which analyzes task-level performance across lightweighting strength, task dynamics, and hardware budget. TISED places policy-intrinsic and execution-aware optimization on a common closed-loop time axis, and separates the end-to-end effect into cycle time, rollout length, and action delay. We use two task regimes throughout the paper. In a static task , the task-relevant environment state remains effectively unchanged during model inference (e.g., static pick & place task). In this regime, lightweighting primarily affects movement smoothness and total execution time. In a dynamic task ,the task-relevant environment state evolves while the model is still computing the next action (e.g., picking a rolling ball or playing ping-pong). In this regime, lightweighting changes both action qual-ity and reaction speed – the inference speed directly affects the policy’s ability to react to dynamic environments. More detailed analysis and derivation can be found in Appendix A.

Action Delay. We also track the residual action delay, i.e., the time from an observation to the first action that can be executed from the corresponding policy output: 

Dbase act = Tinf , Dpl act (α) = αT inf , Dea act (n′) = max {0, T inf − n′Tact }. (6) Dynamic-task analysis further depends on Dact , which captures the observation staleness accumu-lated by the time the corresponding action executes. To cover both policy-intrinsic and execution-aware optimization, let u denote the operating point of the chosen method: u = α for policy-intrinsic 4optimization and u = n′ for execution-aware optimization. We use a structured surrogate to capture the two dominant factors in dynamic success: 

bPsucc (u) = Φ( q(u), z (u)) , z(u) = Dact (u)ωenv , ∂Φ/∂q ≥ 0, ∂Φ/∂z ≤ 0. (7) where q(u) represents the action quality, ωenv is the environment change rate (e.g., conveyor speed or moving-target velocity), z(u) = Dact (u)ωenv measures how far the environment drifts during the action delay, and Φ maps these two factors to a success rate. In words: dynamic success goes up when actions are more accurate, and goes down when the environment has drifted far from what the policy actually saw. As lightweighting strengthens, q(u) typically falls (less computation or staler input) while z(u) may also fall (faster inference shortens the observation-to-action gap). The outcome for dynamic-task success therefore depends on the relative strength of these two trends. 

Overlooked Factor 2 : On dynamic tasks, per-step accuracy does not predict per-task accuracy—reduced observation staleness can outweigh the quality loss. 

Compute Hardware. The timing terms above are not intrinsic to the policy alone. Tinf is deter-mined by the compute platform that runs the policy, whereas Tact is fixed by the robot execution side and the task setting. (1) For static tasks, let ρ = Tinf /T act denote the inference-to-execution time ratio. For a fixed task and a fixed optimization setting, the per-chunk speedups can be written as: 

ηpl chunk (ρ) = ρ + nαρ + n , ηea chunk (ρ) = ρ + nρ + n − min {ρ, n ′} . (8) It means that the same lightweighting setting can yield different task-level effects on different com-pute platforms. (2) For dynamic tasks, the delay Dact can also be impacted by hardware ability, thereby affecting z(u) and bPsucc (u).

Overlooked Factor 3 : Across compute platforms, per-task trends do not generalize—the sweet spot shifts with the hardware’s compute budget. 

4 Experiments 

Section 3 shows that per-step latency is an incomplete proxy for embodied inference optimization. In this section, we empirically characterize how this proxy breaks down through controlled sweeps of lightweighting across task regimes, policy models, optimization methods, and compute platforms. These sweeps are designed to answer three questions: whether per-chunk speedup translates into end-to-end speedup in static tasks, whether reduced action delay translates into better accuracy in dynamic tasks, and whether the shape of the task-level trend and the location of its sweet spot are hardware-dependent. The full experimental combinations, sweep ranges, and implementation details are reported in Appendix B and C. 

4.1 Setup Policy Models. We evaluate policy models at different scales. We use MLP-Mixer [47, 25] and diffusion policy [23] models for simple control tasks, and we also use foundation-scale embodied models in complex environment tasks, including VLAs such as π0.5 [4] and DynamicVLA [48], and WAMs such as Cosmos-Policy [9] and LingBot-VA [10]. 

Optimization Methods. For policy-intrinsic optimization, we use quantization, pruning, and sampling-step reduction to accelerate the model inference. To characterize task-level trends with fine granularity, we construct a series of progressively lightweight operating points for each method to adjust α. For execution-aware optimization, we follow the asynchronous inference settings in VLASH [25] and progressively vary the overlap depth n′.

Simulation Task Setup. We evaluate both task regimes defined in Section 3. For static tasks, we use LIBERO [52], RoboCasa [53], and RoboTwin-2.0 [54]. These benchmarks primarily involve manipulation tasks such as grasping and placing stationary objects, where the task-relevant object states remain effectively unchanged during policy inference. For dynamic tasks, we use Kinetix [55] and DOM [48]. These benchmarks’ task-relevant states continue to evolve during policy inference, and their latency-injection protocols allow us to emulate different inference delays. 

Real-World Task Setup. We further evaluate our observations on a real UR5e robotic arm [56]. In the static setting, the robot needs to pick up a cube on the tabletop and place it in a target box. In the dynamic setting, the robot needs to grasp a cube moving on a conveyor. These two settings share the same manipulation objective but differ in whether the task-relevant object state evolves during policy inference. 

4.2 Empirical Finding 1: A Per-Step Speedup Need Not Speed Up the Static Task 

As shown in Figure 3, across all four model–task pairs, lightweighting—policy-intrinsic ( α) or execution-aware ( n′)—monotonically reduces the per-chunk time Tchunk , but action quality also declines, so the chunk count N grows. Their product Ttask = N ·Tchunk thus depends on which trend dominates. In Figure 3(a), (c), and (d), N stays nearly flat under mild lightweighting and inflates sharply past a task-specific operating point, so Ttask traces a U-shape with an interior minimum below the baseline. Figure 3(b) is the corner case: N inflates from the very first optimization step, so no setting beats the baseline. Figure 3(e) and (f) visualize the underlying mechanism on a representative asynchronous trial: as n′ grows, the robot arm traces longer end-effector paths and makes more failed grasp attempts before completing the task. 

Empirical Finding 1 : On static tasks, Ttask is non-monotonic in lightweighting strength— an interior sweet spot may exist between two regimes: mild lightweighting reduces Ttask via per-chunk speedup, aggressive lightweighting inflates the chunk count N and reverses the gain.

4.4 Empirical Finding 3: The Trend and Optimum Shifts with Hardware 

Empirical Findings 1 and 2 established that a sweet spot may exist on both static and dynamic tasks. We next ask whether its location is hardware-dependent. To isolate the hardware effect, we fix the task and the lightweighting sweep, and vary only the inference hardware, which changes the inference-to-execution ratio ρ = Tinf /T act . Figure 5(a) (static, async sweep over n′): on high-performance hardware ( ρ small), the per-chunk speedup ηchunk is large enough that the gain from inference optimization outweighs the chunk-count inflation, so the sweet spot drifts toward more aggressive overlap; on low-performance hardware ( ρ large), the per-chunk gain shrinks while N

inflates rapidly, so the sweet spot retreats to milder overlap. Figure 5(b) (dynamic, sampling-step sweep over s): on high-performance hardware, inference delay Dact is no longer the dominant factor limiting accuracy, so the sweet spot favors larger s where the policy retains higher action quality 

q; on low-performance hardware, observation staleness z dominates and the policy must trade off action quality against reaction speed, so the sweet spot shifts to smaller s. Across both regimes, the sweet-spot location is qualitatively sensitive to the hardware compute budget; the quantitative derivation is in Appendix A.2.

We conduct two real-world studies on a UR5e robot arm as shown in Figure 6. For the static task, we use a fine-tuned π0.5 model [4] and measure Tinf and Ttask over all successful trials under different sampling-step settings. Table 1a shows that the two metrics exhibit opposite trends as lightweighting becomes more aggressive. For the dynamic experiment, we use a fine-tuned diffusion policy model [23] and compute the score based on the relative position between the gripper and the cube. Table 1c shows that diffusion policies with fewer sampling steps can achieve higher dynamic-task scores, because their faster reaction outweighs the loss in action quality. 5 Conclusion 

We present TISED, a task-level decomposition framework for analyzing lossy inference optimiza-tion in embodied policies. Beyond per-step latency, TISED decomposes optimization into per-chunk speedup, rollout chunk count, action delay, and compute-hardware dependence. Across policy-intrinsic and execution-aware optimization, which validated in both simulation and real-world UR5e experiments, we identify two paradoxes and one drift: (1) faster per-step inference can yield slower 

per-task time on static tasks; (2) less precise inference can yield higher success on dynamic tasks; and (3) the per-task sweet spot drifts with the deployment hardware. More fundamentally, these results elevate closed-loop computation to its own design axis, coupling speed, quality, dynamics, and hardware as joint determinants of task-level performance. 

6 Limitations 

This work has several limitations. (1) Our framework treats per-chunk time and chunk count as the dominant degrees of freedom; second-order effects such as scheduling jitter, and OS-level schedul-ing latency are abstracted away and may matter for more precise analysis. (2) For execution-aware optimization, we model asynchronous inference as a simplified overlap depth, not as a full server-client implementation; production schedulers may exhibit additional queueing or contention effects. (3) For the static tasks, we approximate N as a function of action quality alone; other effects such as motion jerk and grasp stability is observed but not formally modeled. (4) For the dynamic tasks, extremely high-frequency interactions (e.g., table tennis) are out of scope. (5) Our real-world vali-dation is restricted to a single UR5e platform and two task families; large-scale physical validation is left to future work. 8References

17 A.3 Assumptions and Limitations 75 

The analysis above is intentionally structured but lightweight. The analysis above makes several 76 

idealized assumptions. First, quantities such as N pl (α) and N ea (n′) are specified only through 77 

their qualitative trends. Their concrete functional forms are task-dependent and must be obtained 78 

from empirical measurement. Second, Φ is used only as an abstract response function that describes 79 

how dynamic-task success depends on action quality and action delay. We do not assume that Φ has 80 

a fixed parametric form or represents a calibrated physical law. 81

B.2 Task Settings 120 

We group the simulation benchmarks according to the task regimes defined in the main paper: 121 

LIBERO, RoboCasa, and the LeRobot(RoboTwin) tasks are used as static-task benchmarks, while 122 

Kinetix and DOM-CR are used as dynamic-task benchmarks. 123 

LIBERO. LIBERO [52] is a tabletop manipulation benchmark suite designed to evaluate policy 124 

generalization and knowledge transfer across object, spatial, goal, and long-horizon task variations. 125 

We use it as a controlled static-task setting because the task-relevant object and goal states are 126 

assumed to remain unchanged during policy inference. 127 

20 RoboCasa. RoboCasa [53] provides large-scale simulated household manipulation tasks with di-128 

verse kitchen layouts, objects, and task instances. Despite the static-scene assumption, RoboCasa 129 

tasks are typically more challenging than those in LIBERO. 130 

LeRobot. LeRobot [58] is an open-source robot-learning ecosystem that standardizes datasets, 131 

policy training, and evaluation interfaces across simulated and real manipulation tasks. For LingBot-132 

VA, the data and evaluation interface follow the LeRobot-style format, while the underlying simu-133 

lated environments are generated from RoboTwin [54], a SAPIEN-based dual-arm manipulation 134 

benchmark with multi-camera observations and diverse bimanual tasks. These tasks are treated as 135 

static manipulation tasks because task-relevant objects do not deliberately move during policy infer-136 

ence. 137 

Kinetix. Kinetix [55] is a 2D physics-based control benchmark with procedurally designed tasks 138 

involving contact-rich interaction, object manipulation, locomotion, balance, and timing-sensitive 139 

control. The policy is an iterative, flow-style action generator with an MLP-Mixer architecture [25], 140 

and we vary the number of refinement steps in {1, 3, 5} to trade off zero-delay action quality against 141 

inference latency. We evaluate both zero-delay inference, denoted as inf-hw in the main text, 142 

and hardware-induced delay. For the latter, we profile inference latency τ and map it to an inte-143 

ger control-step delay d = round( τ / 33 .33ms) , since Kinetix applies actions every two 16.67 ms 144 

physics steps. Consequently, different profiled latencies can fall into the same simulator delay bin. 145

C Real-World Experiment Details (Section 4.5) 164 

We use a UR5e robot arm [56] to validate the two task regimes in the physical world. The real-165 

world robot setup follows UMI [59], with the mirrors on the gripper removed. The setup consists of 166 

a UR5e robot arm, a UMI gripper, and a GoPro camera for RGB observation. All experiments were 167 

conducted with the same robot arm speed setting of 30% and a control frequency of 10 Hz. 168 

To evaluate how compute capability, i.e., ρ, affects Ttask or task accuracy, we first profile 169 

the policy model’s inference latency Tinf across different hardware platforms. We then intro-170 

duce a hardware-latency scaling factor λ on the RTX 4070 platform, i.e., Ttask ,simulated =171 

λT task ,original , ρ simulated = λρ original , which allows us to simulate the inference behavior of dif-172 

ferent compute platforms under the same task environment. 173 

C.1 Static Experiment Settings 174 

We collected 198 expert demonstrations using the UMI gripper and fine-tuned the π0.5 [4] model 175 

for 30,000 training steps using LoRA [60]. During LoRA fine-tuning, we initialized the policy from 176 

the π0.5-base model, which consists of two components: a VLM backbone and an action expert. 177 

The VLM backbone is PaliGemma [14], composed of a SigLIP vision encoder and a Gemma-2B 178 

language model, while the action expert is a 300M Gemma model. We fine-tuned both components 179 

with a learning rate of 2.5 × 10 −5 and a batch size of 32. 180 

The static manipulation task requires the robot to pick up a red cube on the tabletop and place it into 181 

a target box. A trial is considered successful only if the gripper fingers open and release the cube 182 

22 into the target box. Each rollout started from the same predefined initial position. For each rollout 183 

setting, we conducted 5 trials and reported the corresponding task performance. 184 

C.2 Dynamic Experiment Settings 185 

We collected 196 expert demonstrations using the UMI gripper and trained the diffusion policy [23] 186 

for 130 epochs. The diffusion policy model is composed of a pretrained ViT vision encoder and a 187 

DiT action decoder. The DiT module was trained from scratch with a learning rate of 3.0 × 10 −4.188 

For the visual encoder, we initialized the ViT backbone from the CLIP [61] pretrained ViT-L/14 189 

model [62] and trained it with a learning rate of 3.0 × 10 −5 and a batch size of 32. 190 

The dynamic manipulation task requires the robot to grasp the red cube as it moves on a conveyor. 191 

We evaluate grasping performance using a three-level score: 2 indicates that the red cube is suc-192 

cessfully grasped and held by both gripper fingers; 1 indicates that only one gripper finger contacts 193 

the red cube; and 0 indicates that neither gripper finger reaches the red cube. This scoring criterion 194 

reports both the robot’s reaction speed and motion quality in the dynamic task. The conveyor moved 195 

at a constant speed of 6.25 cm/s during evaluation. For each rollout setting, we conducted 30 trials. 196 

Specifically, we used three fixed initial positions and performed 10 trials for each position. 197 

D Additional Discussions 198 

D.1 About “Successful-Only Trial’s Chunks” 199 

Following the successful-episode reporting protocol in prior work [45], we report chunk number and 200 

completion time only over successful rollouts: 201 

Nsucc = E[N | success] , Ttask ,succ = E[Ttask | success] . (31) The successful-only convention avoids an artifact of simulator rollouts: when a task fails, the sim-202 

ulator may continue executing additional chunks and corrective attempts until the rollout horizon is 203 

reached. Including timeout trajectories would inflate N and Ttask , so the averages would reflect the 204 

simulator termination rule instead of the efficiency of completed executions. 205
